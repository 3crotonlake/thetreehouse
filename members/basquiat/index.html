Jean-Michel Basquiat was born in Brooklyn in 1960 to a Haitian father and Puerto Rican mother. At seven, he was struck by a car and spent weeks in hospital, where his mother brought him a copy of Grey's Anatomy — a book that would shape his visual language for the rest of his life.

He dropped out of school at 17 and began tagging Lower Manhattan walls as SAMO© — Same Old Shit — with a friend. The tags were aphorisms, critiques, poetry: "SAMO© as an end to mindwash religion, nowhere politics and bogus philosophy." They were noticed. By 1982 he was showing internationally and selling major works.

Basquiat worked fast, in Armani suits spattered with paint, across canvas, paper, doors, refrigerators. His drawings in particular — dense with crossed-out words, anatomical diagrams, crowns, and figures — are among the most electrically charged works on paper of the 20th century. He died in 1988 at 27.
