Pablo Picasso was born in Málaga, Spain in 1881 and died in Mougins, France in 1973. He co-founded Cubism, invented collage as a fine art medium, and was among the most prolific artists in history — producing an estimated 20,000 works across painting, drawing, sculpture, ceramics, and printmaking.

The 347 Series was made between March and October 1968, when Picasso was 86 years old. Working with master printers Aldo and Piero Crommelynck at a press near his Mougins studio, he produced 347 etchings in seven months. The prints teem with musketeers, circus performers, painters and their models, mythological figures — all observed with the eye of a novelist. Picasso himself described it as "my way of writing fiction."

The series was first exhibited at the Art Institute of Chicago and the Galerie Louise Leiris in Paris in 1970. It remains one of the most remarkable late-career achievements in the history of printmaking.
